Research

Research Interests

Quantum Computing · High-Performance Computing · Machine Learning · Numerical Methods · Robotics

My work sits at the intersection of algorithmic theory and systems-level implementation. I am interested in problems where the gap between what is theoretically possible and what runs efficiently on real hardware is the central challenge — and in closing that gap through careful design at every level of the stack.

Quantum Algorithms & Quantum Computing

My primary research interest lies in quantum computing — specifically, in algorithms that offer provable advantages over classical methods for computationally hard problems. I have worked with quantum linear algebra through the Krylov Quantum Diagonalization framework, Hamiltonian simulation techniques including Linear Combination of Hamiltonian Simulation (LCHS) and Linear Combination of Unitaries (LCU), and foundational quantum algorithms including Grover's search, Shor's factoring algorithm, and quantum error correction codes.

I am particularly interested in the boundary between near-term NISQ devices and fault-tolerant computation — what can realistically be demonstrated on today's hardware, and what the path to practical quantum advantage looks like. My LCHS project involved running circuits on real IBM Quantum hardware, which gave me direct experience with the gap between noiseless simulation and physical device behaviour.

High-Performance Computing & GPU Programming

I believe that algorithmic insight without efficient implementation is incomplete. My HPC work centres on writing hand-tuned CUDA kernels for quantum statevector simulation, designing distributed multi-GPU systems using CUDA-aware MPI, and exploiting low-level architectural features — shared-memory tree reductions, fused two-qubit gate kernels, coalesced memory access, and statevector reuse — to close the gap between theoretical and hardware performance.

A motivating question behind my KQD project was whether hand-written CUDA could decisively outperform a high-level abstraction framework (NVIDIA CUDA-Q) for the same algorithm. The answer was yes — 6.2× at 23 qubits, with the gap widening as problem size grows. This reinforced my conviction that for performance-critical scientific computing, systems-level programming remains essential even as higher-level tools mature.

Machine Learning

My ML interests span both theory and implementation. I have built core algorithms entirely from scratch — Gaussian Discriminant Analysis, Logistic Regression, Poisson Regression via the Generalised Linear Model framework, feedforward and convolutional neural networks, Naive Bayes with Laplace smoothing, Support Vector Machines in primal and dual form, and Gaussian Mixture Models via EM — and applied them to benchmark datasets including MNIST, MakeMoons, Delhi air quality time series, and medical image classification.

I have also worked with more advanced architectures: Vision Transformers implemented from scratch (multi-head self-attention, positional encoding, patch embedding), LSTMs and GRUs for temporal data, and generative models including DCGANs, WGAN-GP, and Variational Autoencoders. I am interested in the mathematical foundations of learning: generalisation bounds, optimisation landscapes, and understanding why particular architectures and training procedures work at a level deeper than empirical observation.

Numerical Methods for Differential Equations

I am interested in numerical methods for PDEs — finite difference schemes, Gaussian quadrature, spectral methods, Krylov subspace iterative solvers — and in emerging quantum-classical hybrid approaches to solving differential equations. The LCHS project sits at this intersection: the problem is classical (1D diffusion PDE), the solution method is quantum (Hamiltonian simulation as a proxy for matrix exponentiation), and the validation is spectral (Fourier mode decomposition comparing quantum against classical FTCS finite difference). This interplay between numerical analysis and quantum algorithms is an area I want to develop further.

Robotics & Computer Vision

I have worked on visual simultaneous localisation and mapping (SLAM) combined with robotic manipulation, resulting in a publication at CVIP 2024. My interest in robotics is shaped by the challenge of building systems that must perceive, reason, and act reliably under uncertainty — where sensor noise, computational constraints, and physical dynamics interact continuously. The problem of building an accurate internal model of the world from imperfect observations, and using that model to act, connects deeply to questions in probabilistic inference and numerical methods that run through all my work.
