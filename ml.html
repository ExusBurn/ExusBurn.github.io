Assignment 1 — Classical Machine Learning from Scratch

The first assignment covers the foundational algorithms of statistical machine learning, implemented entirely from NumPy and SciPy — no scikit-learn, no high-level ML libraries. The goal is to understand these methods at the mathematical level and to appreciate the computational challenges that motivated later developments.

Ordinary Least Squares Regression

OLS regression is derived and implemented in its closed-form solution (β = (XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀy) and compared against iterative gradient descent. The numerical issues of forming XᵀX directly — condition number blow-up, catastrophic cancellation — motivate the use of QR decomposition for a stable implementation.

Support Vector Machines

Both the primal and dual formulations of the SVM are derived. The dual is solved as a quadratic program using SciPy's optimisation routines. The kernel trick is implemented for RBF, polynomial, and linear kernels, and the KKT conditions are used to extract support vectors explicitly. Decision boundaries are visualised in 2D for interpretability.

Logistic Regression

Binary and multi-class logistic regression via one-vs-rest and softmax formulations. Gradient descent and Newton's method are compared. The probabilistic interpretation (logistic regression as a conditional likelihood model) is emphasised alongside the discriminative boundary view.

Gaussian Mixture Models & EM

GMMs are fitted via the Expectation-Maximisation algorithm, derived step by step from the evidence lower bound (ELBO). The assignment explores the effect of the number of components, covariance structure (spherical, diagonal, full), and initialisation sensitivity. The connection between EM and coordinate ascent in variational inference is made explicit.

Naïve Bayes

Gaussian Naïve Bayes and Bernoulli Naïve Bayes are implemented from likelihood principles. The conditional independence assumption is tested empirically, and the effect of Laplace smoothing on categorical features is studied.

Vectorised KNN: 94× Speedup

K-Nearest Neighbours is a deceptively simple algorithm whose naïve implementation is catastrophically slow for large datasets due to its O(n²d) pairwise distance computation. The assignment implements a fully vectorised version using NumPy broadcasting that computes all pairwise distances in a single matrix operation:

‖xᵢ − xⱼ‖² = ‖xᵢ‖² − 2xᵢ·xⱼ + ‖xⱼ‖²

This can be computed for all (i,j) pairs simultaneously using outer products and batch matrix multiplications — operations that are heavily optimised in NumPy's BLAS backend. The result is a 94× speedup over a naive Python loop implementation on the benchmark dataset.

Assignment 2 — Deep Learning with PyTorch

The second assignment moves into modern deep learning, implementing a suite of architectures in PyTorch and applying them to three distinct real-world datasets, each with different input modalities and prediction challenges.

Datasets

  • Delhi Air Quality Time Series: Multivariate time-series data of air pollutant concentrations in Delhi, used for sequence modelling and forecasting tasks.
  • PlantVillage Crop Disease: A large image dataset of plant leaves labelled with 38 disease classes, used for image classification. Models must generalise across crop species and disease severity.
  • APTOS Retinal Fundus Images: Medical imaging dataset of retinal photographs graded for diabetic retinopathy severity (0–4 scale). Poses a fine-grained ordinal regression challenge.

Multilayer Perceptrons

The assignment begins with fully-connected feedforward networks, studying the effect of depth, width, activation functions, and regularisation (L2, dropout, batch normalisation) on generalisation. Vanishing gradients are explicitly demonstrated — training curves and gradient norm histories for very deep MLPs without normalisation show how signal fails to propagate to early layers.

Convolutional Neural Networks

CNNs are applied to the image datasets. Architectures are designed from scratch rather than using pretrained models. The assignment studies the effect of receptive field size, pooling strategies, and skip connections (ResNet-style) on classification accuracy. Grad-CAM visualisations are used to inspect which image regions activate each filter.

Recurrent Networks: RNNs, LSTMs, GRUs

Recurrent architectures are applied to the Delhi air quality time series. Vanilla RNNs are shown to fail on long sequences due to vanishing gradients — an empirical demonstration of the issue that motivated LSTMs. LSTM and GRU are then implemented and compared, with attention to the gating mechanisms that allow them to selectively retain information across long temporal horizons.

Transformers & Vision Transformers

The Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) is implemented from scratch: multi-head self-attention, positional encoding, feedforward sublayers, and layer normalisation. The attention mechanism is then adapted to images via the Vision Transformer (ViT) — the image is patchified into a sequence of tokens, and a standard Transformer encoder processes the resulting sequence.

The assignment includes a study of how attention patterns in ViT differ from those in NLP Transformers — early layers attend locally (similar to CNNs) while deeper layers develop global attention patterns that span the entire image.

Key result: FID Score Computation

The Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) is implemented and used to evaluate generative quality — even in the discriminative setting, FID between real and augmented data provides a useful diagnostic. Understanding FID requires working with the statistics of deep feature spaces and computing the Fréchet distance between two Gaussian distributions.

Assignment 3 — Generative Models & Reinforcement Learning

The third assignment covers the frontier of modern ML: ensemble methods, generative modelling, self-supervised learning, and policy gradient reinforcement learning.

Ensemble Methods: Random Forests & Gradient Boosting

Random Forests are implemented using bagging over decision tree base learners, with feature subsampling at each split for decorrelation. Gradient Boosted Trees (GBT) are derived from the functional gradient descent perspective — each tree is fit to the residuals of the current ensemble. The assignment compares the two approaches on the same datasets, examining bias-variance tradeoffs and calibration.

Generative Adversarial Networks

GANs are implemented in PyTorch with both a standard DCGAN architecture and a conditional variant. Training instability is a central theme: mode collapse, the vanishing gradient problem in the discriminator, and the importance of careful learning rate scheduling are all studied empirically. The Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) is implemented as a more stable alternative, with gradient penalty (WGAN-GP) as the constraint mechanism.

Variational Autoencoders

VAEs are derived from first principles via the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO). The reparameterisation trick — the critical innovation that allows gradients to flow through a stochastic sampling step — is implemented and its role in making the latent space continuous and interpolatable is demonstrated. The assignment includes latent space visualisations showing how the model organises different classes.

SimCLR — Self-Supervised Contrastive Learning

SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020) is implemented from scratch: two views of each image are created via random augmentation (crop, colour jitter, blur), and a contrastive loss (NT-Xent) is minimised to attract representations of the same image and repel those of different images. The resulting encoder, trained with no labels, achieves competitive accuracy when a linear classifier is added on top. This demonstrates that self-supervised learning can extract rich semantic representations from unlabelled data.

REINFORCE — Policy Gradient RL

The REINFORCE algorithm (Williams, 1992) is implemented and applied to a standard control environment. The core challenge of policy gradient methods — high variance in the gradient estimate — is addressed via a baseline subtraction (advantage function). The assignment includes learning curves, episode reward distributions, and analysis of how baseline choice affects sample efficiency.
