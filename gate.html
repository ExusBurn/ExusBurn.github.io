How I Got into IISc CDS — GATE Preparation Guide

For students serious about research in India's premier institutions

This page documents my complete top-down approach to GATE preparation and the path to IISc CDS. The accompanying video is a detailed, unfiltered walkthrough — what I did, what worked, what I would change, and what it actually takes to compete at the highest level.

Why IISc CDS?

The Indian Institute of Science is one of a very small number of institutions in India where graduate students do research that is genuinely competitive at the international level. The Department of Computational and Data Sciences, in particular, occupies a rare position: it is explicitly interdisciplinary, sitting at the intersection of mathematics, computer science, and domain-specific scientific computing.

If you want to work on quantum computing, high-performance simulation, machine learning theory, or computational biology in India — at a place that takes research seriously, provides real resources, and connects you to the global research community — IISc CDS is one of the best options available.

But it is genuinely hard to get in. GATE is not a formality — you need a high score in a competitive field, and the written test and interview are designed to filter for research aptitude, not just exam performance.

My Top-Down Approach

Most GATE preparation advice is bottom-up: start with the syllabus, go subject by subject, solve previous year papers at the end. I took a different approach — starting from the end goal (the IISc interview) and working backwards.

Step 1: Understand what IISc actually wants

The interview at IISc CDS is not a GATE re-examination. They want to see that you can think — that you understand concepts deeply enough to apply them in new contexts, that you are genuinely curious, and that you have some idea of what research involves. This completely changes what you should study and how.

Step 2: Map the GATE syllabus to depth, not breadth

GATE CS covers a lot of ground. The mistake most people make is treating every topic equally. I categorised topics by: (a) weight in the exam, (b) conceptual depth required for IISc interview, and (c) foundational importance for future study. Topics like Algorithms, Discrete Mathematics, Linear Algebra, and Probability got deep treatment. Some peripheral topics got surface coverage.

Step 3: Understand first, memorise never

GATE questions — especially the hard ones — are designed to reward understanding over memorisation. I spent significantly more time understanding proofs, working through why algorithms are correct, and deriving results from first principles than on rote revision. This paid off both in the exam and in the interview.

Step 4: Previous year papers are the actual syllabus

The GATE paper is not random. Certain question types, problem patterns, and conceptual areas repeat with high frequency. Solving the last 10 years of GATE CS papers — carefully, with full understanding of each solution — gives you a much better picture of what actually matters than reading the official syllabus document.

Step 5: Interview preparation is separate

A high GATE score gets you an interview call. What happens in the interview is a different kind of preparation: mathematical maturity, the ability to think on your feet, and genuine curiosity about research problems. I prepared by reading research papers in areas I was interested in, working through problems from textbooks rather than coaching materials, and practising explaining ideas clearly.

Resources I Actually Used

I am deliberate about not recommending resources I did not personally use. The following are what I found genuinely valuable — all discussed in more detail in the video.

  • CLRS (Introduction to Algorithms): The definitive algorithms reference. Not every chapter — but the core chapters on sorting, graph algorithms, dynamic programming, and NP-completeness are essential reading for the interview, not just the exam.
  • Rosen's Discrete Mathematics: Thorough on combinatorics, graph theory, and logic. Useful for building the kind of mathematical maturity the interview tests for.
  • Gilbert Strang's Linear Algebra: The best accessible treatment of linear algebra for someone without a pure mathematics background. The lecture videos (MIT OpenCourseWare) are exceptional.
  • GATE PYQs (previous year questions): The indispensable preparation tool. Solve them timed, review every mistake, understand every solution.
  • Standard coaching material (selectively): Useful for structured topic coverage, but should supplement deep understanding rather than replace it.

What Nobody Tells You

A few things I wish someone had told me earlier:

  • Your rank matters, but your interview matters more for IISc. A very high rank without research aptitude will not get you in. The interview is designed to find this out quickly.
  • Start preparation earlier than you think you need to. The depth of understanding GATE rewards — and the IISc interview expects — cannot be compressed into a few months of intensive study. Eighteen months of steady, deep preparation is better than six months of exhausting cramming.
  • Choosing IISc CDS means choosing research. If you want a degree primarily for placement, there are better choices. If you want to spend two years thinking hard about difficult problems, learning from world-class researchers, and building skills that matter in the long run — this is the place.
  • The first semester will be hard. See the first semester page. Go in with realistic expectations and a willingness to work.
