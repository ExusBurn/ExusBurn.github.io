Research Interests

Quantum Algorithms & Quantum Computing

My primary research interest lies in quantum computing — specifically, in algorithms that offer provable advantages over classical methods for computationally hard problems. I have worked with quantum linear algebra (Krylov subspace methods adapted for quantum hardware), Hamiltonian simulation techniques (LCHS, LCU), and foundational quantum algorithms including Grover's search, Shor's factoring, and quantum error correction. I am particularly interested in the boundary between near-term NISQ devices and fault-tolerant computation, and in understanding what quantum speedups are practically achievable today.

High-Performance Computing & GPU Programming

I believe that algorithmic insight without efficient implementation is incomplete. My HPC work focuses on writing hand-tuned CUDA kernels for quantum statevector simulation, designing distributed multi-GPU systems using CUDA-aware MPI, and exploiting architectural features — shared-memory reductions, coalesced memory access, fused kernels — to close the gap between theoretical and hardware performance. I am interested in cases where low-level systems programming decisively outperforms high-level framework abstractions.

Machine Learning

My ML interests span both theory and implementation. I have built core algorithms from scratch — Gaussian Discriminant Analysis, Logistic Regression, Poisson Regression via GLM, feedforward and convolutional neural networks, Naive Bayes with Laplace smoothing — and applied them to benchmark tasks including digit recognition (MNIST), binary classification (MakeMoons), and regression. I am interested in the mathematical foundations of learning: generalisation bounds, optimisation landscapes, and why particular architectures work.

Numerical Methods for Differential Equations

I am interested in numerical methods for PDEs — finite difference schemes, Gaussian quadrature, spectral methods — and in emerging quantum-classical hybrid approaches to solving differential equations. My LCHS project sits at this intersection: implementing a quantum algorithm (Linear Combination of Hamiltonian Simulation) to solve the 1D diffusion equation and comparing against classical FTCS finite difference, with rigorous spectral error analysis across Fourier modes.

Robotics & Computer Vision

Distributed Krylov Quantum Diagonalization

Quantum ComputingCUDA C++CUDA-Aware MPI OpenMPcuBLASLAPACK

Final project, Introduction to Scalable Systems (Parallel Programming) · IISc CDS, 2025–26

Designed and implemented a fully distributed, GPU-accelerated system for finding ground state energies of quantum spin systems via the Krylov Quantum Diagonalization (KQD) algorithm. The algorithm builds a Krylov subspace — span{|ψ₀⟩, H|ψ₀⟩, H²|ψ₀⟩, …} — from an initial quantum state and iteratively refines the ground state energy estimate by solving a small generalized eigenvalue problem H·c = E·S·c classically via LAPACK.

The implementation targets the Heisenberg XXX spin chain, representing quantum states as complex statevectors of length 2ⁿ in GPU memory and applying Pauli interactions as hand-written CUDA kernels. Key optimisations include fused two-qubit gate kernels (reducing memory passes per interaction), shared-memory tree reductions for inner products, statevector reuse across Krylov iterations, and coalesced memory access patterns. For multi-GPU runs, the statevector is partitioned across devices using CUDA-aware MPI, enabling direct GPU-to-GPU transfers without CPU staging.

  • 6.2× speedup over NVIDIA CUDA-Q baseline at 23 qubits — the gap widens with qubit count as framework abstraction overhead scales with problem size
  • Near-ideal strong scaling from 1 to 3 GPUs — doubling GPUs roughly halves runtime, demonstrating that MPI communication overhead is negligible relative to computation
  • CPU reference implementation in OpenMP + MPI for correctness verification and baseline performance measurement

Quantum Solution of the Heat Equation (LCHS)

Quantum ComputingQiskitPython NumPySciPy

Final project, Numerical Solution of Differential Equations · IISc CDS, 2025–26

Implemented the Linear Combination of Hamiltonian Simulation (LCHS) algorithm in Qiskit to solve the one-dimensional diffusion equation ∂u/∂t = α(t)·∂²u/∂x² on a periodic domain. The spatial domain is discretised into 16 grid points (4 qubits); time integration uses 64 Gauss–Legendre quadrature nodes, each corresponding to a Hamiltonian simulation circuit weighted by its quadrature coefficient.

The quantum circuit consists of a state preparation stage, a V-PREPARE block loading quadrature weights across 6 ancilla qubits, a SELECT unitary applying controlled Hamiltonian simulations, and an UNPREP stage to project onto the desired linear combination. Results were validated against a classical Forward-Time Centered-Space (FTCS) finite difference solver and analysed spectrally — LCHS accurately reproduces low-frequency Fourier modes, with relative error increasing for high-frequency modes due to quadrature truncation.
