Pattern Recognition & Neural Networks

PRNN is the flagship course of the second semester — broad, rigorous, and demanding. It covers the full arc of machine learning, from classical statistical theory to cutting-edge deep learning, with heavy emphasis on implementation.

The course is structured around three major assignments. Each builds on the last, and together they represent a comprehensive survey of the field. See the dedicated Machine Learning page for a full writeup of each assignment, including classical ML from scratch, deep learning architectures, and generative models.

Parallel Programming

The Parallel Programming course is where software meets hardware. It covers three major paradigms of parallel computation, each targeting a different level of the memory hierarchy:

  • CUDA (GPU computing): Writing kernels that exploit the massive parallelism of GPU hardware. Thread blocks, warps, shared memory, memory coalescing, occupancy tuning, and the CUDA execution model. The course goes well beyond using libraries — students write their own kernels for matrix multiplication, reductions, and stencil operations.
  • MPI (distributed memory): Message Passing Interface for computations that span multiple nodes or GPUs. Point-to-point communication, collective operations (broadcast, scatter, gather, reduce, allreduce), and CUDA-aware MPI for direct GPU-to-GPU transfers without CPU staging.
  • OpenMP (shared memory): Thread-level parallelism within a single node. Pragmas, race conditions, synchronisation, and performance tradeoffs versus explicit threading.

The final project — Distributed Krylov Quantum Diagonalization — combines all three: CUDA kernels for GPU acceleration, MPI for multi-GPU distribution, and OpenMP in the CPU reference port. See the KQD page for the full writeup.

Numerical Solutions of Differential Equations

NSDE covers the classical and modern methods for numerically solving ODEs and PDEs — the mathematical backbone of simulation in physics, engineering, and scientific computing.

  • Finite Difference Methods (FDM): Discretising derivatives on a grid. Forward-time / backward-time / Crank-Nicolson schemes. Stability analysis via von Neumann stability theory. CFL conditions. Convergence rates and truncation error.
  • Finite Element Methods (FEM): Weak formulations, Galerkin projection, basis function choice (hat functions, higher-order polynomials). Assembly of stiffness and mass matrices. Application to elliptic, parabolic, and hyperbolic PDEs.
  • Spectral Methods: Fourier and Chebyshev expansions for periodic and non-periodic domains. Spectral accuracy (exponential convergence for smooth solutions). Pseudospectral methods and the FFT.
  • Krylov Iterative Solvers: Conjugate Gradient, GMRES, and their preconditioned variants for large sparse linear systems arising from PDE discretisation.

The final project uses quantum computing (Qiskit) to solve the heat equation via the LCHS algorithm — an explicit bridge between the classical numerical methods of the course and the emerging field of quantum-accelerated numerical analysis. See the LCHS page for the complete writeup.

Natural Language Processing

The NLP course covers the major methods in language understanding and generation, from classical approaches to modern large language models. Topics include tokenisation and text preprocessing, language models (n-gram through neural), word embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe), sequence-to-sequence models, attention mechanisms, and the Transformer architecture as applied to NLP tasks.

The course provides both the theoretical grounding (information-theoretic views of language modelling, probabilistic sequence models) and hands-on experience fine-tuning pretrained models for downstream tasks.

Reflection

The semester in brief

The second semester is where the first semester pays off — and where the real cost of ambition becomes visible. Almost all of my coursework this semester landed at the intersection of quantum computing, which I had to learn from scratch while keeping up with four demanding courses. In effect, I ran six courses' worth of work. The result was a semester under considerable pressure that ended better than expected.

My tip for future students: this semester is about taking the mathematical foundations of the first semester and building them into something real. Actually use the math in your research. The courses are not isolated — they reinforce each other, and the projects are where that interconnection becomes tangible.

Parallel Programming

The most technically demanding course in the programme — and the most fun. The learning curve on CUDA is steep, the assignments expose every inefficiency in your thinking about memory and parallelism, and the final project tests whether you understood the principles or just the syntax. I was borderline failing at midsem. The final project — the Distributed KQD implementation — ended up being the strongest in the batch. That turnaround is something I'm proud of, and I'm developing it toward a publication.

Numerical Solutions of Differential Equations

NSDE pushed into equally novel territory. The final project — solving the heat equation via the LCHS quantum algorithm in Qiskit — required reading research papers on quantum simulation that had almost no overlap with the course curriculum. It was, in effect, a sixth course. That kind of work is hard to manage inside a normal semester schedule, but the intellectual payoff was real. NSDE and PP together pushed me further in quantum computing than any formal quantum course would have.

Pattern Recognition & Neural Networks

PRNN is the best ML course at IISc, by a significant margin. The professor approaches the subject from the mathematics outward — deriving why algorithms work, not just showing how to call them. Assignments are long and genuinely demanding. The exam and assignment schedule can feel lazy. But sitting in class is worthwhile regardless of the grade. If you're serious about understanding machine learning at a mathematical level, take PRNN.

Natural Language Processing

NLP is broader and more theoretical than mathematical. The exam tests recall and conceptual understanding more than derivation, which wasn't what I was prepared for. Seniors often recommend it for placement preparation — the coverage of language models, attention, and transformers is useful context. But for depth of ML understanding, PRNN is irreplaceable. NLP is a reasonable complement, not a substitute.

On internship preparation

January was internship season and it dominated attention for the first month of the semester. I'd prepared after the first semester — projects on SMOTE for Mastercard, and basic quantum algorithms for Fujitsu Research. The first Mastercard interview covered logistic regression, loss functions, and SMOTE; the second asked how to handle class imbalance by adjusting gradients directly, and a question on knowledge distillation from a hard-label to a soft-label predictor.

My recommendation: get the internship, even at a cost to your GPA. The grade hit is real — midsem suffered, and reading unrelated quantum papers while preparing for placements compressed the entire semester. But the mental security of having placement sorted early is worth more than the GPA points it costs. The stress of going without an internship would have been far more damaging than the grades I gave up. Lesser grades with an internship beats no internship with better grades, every time.

On using AI during research

Claude was genuinely useful for breaking down complex research papers and explaining algorithms at the right level of abstraction. It was not useful for writing novel research code — if you're implementing something with no documentation or prior examples, AI will produce plausible-looking code that doesn't do what you need. The workflow that actually worked: understand the algorithm yourself, write the code yourself, then use AI to verify your logic and check your syntax. Treat it as a reviewer of your reasoning, not a generator of it.
