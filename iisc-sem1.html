First Semester at IISc CDS

Department of Computational and Data Sciences  ·  2025  ·  GitHub Repository →

The first semester at IISc CDS is designed to establish a rigorous mathematical and computational foundation. Courses span linear algebra, probability, algorithms, and the theory of computation — building the common language that underpins everything from machine learning to quantum computing.

Context: What IISc CDS Is

The Department of Computational and Data Sciences at IISc is unlike most computer science or mathematics departments. It is explicitly interdisciplinary — its faculty work on scientific computing, machine learning theory, visualisation, computational biology, and quantum information, often in the same group. The curriculum reflects this: it demands both mathematical rigour and the ability to implement and experiment.

Getting into CDS requires clearing the GATE examination at a high level, followed by a written test and interview. The programme is designed for students who want to do research, not just apply techniques. The first semester sets the intellectual standard that the rest of the programme builds on.

Courses

The first semester at IISc CDS comprises five core courses, each taught at a level that assumes strong undergraduate preparation in mathematics and computing. Together they establish the theoretical and computational vocabulary for everything that follows.

Numerical Linear Algebra (NLA)

NLA is the backbone of scientific computing. The course begins with the fundamentals — floating-point arithmetic, conditioning, stability, and the cost of basic operations — before moving to the major matrix factorisation techniques.

  • QR Factorisation: Gram–Schmidt and Householder reflections. QR is the workhorse of stable least squares and the foundation of eigenvalue algorithms. The classical Gram–Schmidt instability and the superiority of modified Gram–Schmidt and Householder variants are studied in numerical detail.
  • SVD (Singular Value Decomposition): The fundamental decomposition of numerical linear algebra. Derivation, geometric interpretation, and applications: low-rank approximation, pseudoinverses, PCA, and condition number analysis. The SVD explains the geometry of least squares and why some systems are inherently hard to solve.
  • Power Iterations & Eigenvalue Methods: The power method, inverse iteration, Rayleigh quotient iteration, and the QR algorithm for full eigendecomposition. Convergence rates, deflation, and the shift-and-invert trick for targeting specific parts of the spectrum.
  • Krylov Subspace Methods: Lanczos iteration for symmetric matrices, Arnoldi for non-symmetric, and GMRES for linear systems. The connection between Krylov methods and polynomial approximation theory — why these methods converge fast on matrices with clustered spectra. This material directly underpins the KQD project in the second semester.

Stochastic Models and Applications (StoMa)

A rigorous treatment of probability theory and stochastic processes, beginning from the measure-theoretic foundations and building toward modern applications in simulation and analysis.

  • Measure-theoretic probability: Probability spaces, σ-algebras, the Borel σ-algebra on ℝ, measurable functions, Lebesgue integration. This is the formal foundation on which all of probability theory rests — understanding why the Borel σ-algebra is the right structure for real-valued random variables.
  • Markov Chains: Discrete-time and continuous-time Markov chains, transition matrices, stationary distributions, ergodicity, and mixing times. The Perron–Frobenius theorem and its implications for convergence. Applications to Monte Carlo methods and queueing theory.
  • Stochastic Processes: Autocorrelation and autocovariance functions, wide-sense stationarity, power spectral density, and the Wiener–Khinchin theorem. Poisson processes, Brownian motion, and Gaussian processes as the continuous-time limit of random walks.

Numerical Methods (NM)

A focused course on the practical numerical methods that appear throughout scientific computing, with emphasis on implementation and error analysis.

  • Newton's Method: Root finding in one and multiple dimensions. Convergence analysis — quadratic convergence near a simple root, linear convergence at repeated roots. Variants: secant method, Broyden's method for systems.
  • Numerical Differentiation: Finite difference approximations to derivatives (forward, backward, central). Truncation error and the tradeoff between step size and floating-point cancellation. Richardson extrapolation for improved accuracy.
  • Numerical Integration: Newton–Cotes rules (trapezoidal, Simpson), Gaussian quadrature (Gauss–Legendre, Gauss–Hermite), and adaptive integration. Error bounds and convergence for smooth vs. non-smooth integrands. This directly connects to the Gauss–Legendre nodes used in the LCHS project.

Introduction to Data Science (IDS)

A rigorous course bridging statistics and machine learning, with substantial overlap with StoMa in probabilistic foundations but going further into the data analysis direction.

  • Autocorrelation & Autocovariance (in depth): More rigorous treatment than StoMa — sample estimators, bias, and the effect of finite data. Spectral estimation via periodograms and Welch's method. Hypothesis testing for stationarity.
  • Statistical Estimation: MLE, MAP, Bayesian inference, confidence intervals, and hypothesis testing. The Cramér–Rao bound as the fundamental limit on estimator variance.
  • Machine Learning: Linear regression, regularisation (ridge, lasso), classification (logistic regression, SVMs), dimensionality reduction (PCA), and model selection (cross-validation, information criteria). Emphasis on the probabilistic interpretation of each method.

Introduction to Scalable Systems

A course on the systems foundations required for large-scale computing — understanding how modern hardware actually works and how to design software that exploits it.

  • Memory hierarchies: Cache behaviour, locality of reference, TLB, and the performance impact of memory access patterns. Why an algorithm's memory access pattern can matter as much as its asymptotic complexity.
  • Parallelism fundamentals: Threading models, race conditions, synchronisation primitives, and Amdahl's law. Introduction to GPU architectures as a motivating example of massively parallel hardware — setting the stage for the second-semester Parallel Programming course.
  • Distributed computing basics: MapReduce, consistency models, and the CAP theorem. How to reason about correctness and performance in systems where state is spread across multiple machines.

Reflection

The first semester is demanding by design. The workload is high and the material moves quickly — it assumes strong undergraduate preparation and rewards those who engage deeply rather than trying to memorise. What it provides in return is a set of mental tools that make every subsequent course easier to understand at a fundamental level.

What the five courses share is a common emphasis on rigour, derivation, and depth over breadth. NLA builds the machinery that makes the Krylov methods in KQD possible. StoMa provides the formal foundations for everything probabilistic in ML. Numerical Methods gives you the quadrature theory that appears directly in the LCHS project. The courses are not isolated — they form a tightly connected intellectual foundation.

For anyone preparing to enter IISc CDS: the first semester will be the most significant adjustment you experience. The transition from undergraduate to graduate-level rigour is real. See the GATE preparation page for more on how to get here and what to expect.
