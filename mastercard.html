The Problem: Sequential Recommendation

Classical collaborative filtering asks a static question: given the set of things this user liked, what else will they like? It treats your history as a bag. Order does not matter. Watching Memento then Inception is the same as watching Inception then Memento.

That is obviously wrong. Order carries enormous signal. Someone who just finished the first three episodes of a series wants episode four, not a thematically similar show. Someone who watched three thrillers in a row this week is in a different mood than the same person was a month ago. Sequential recommendation reframes the task as next-item prediction: given an ordered sequence of interactions s = [i1, i2, …, it], predict it+1.

Written that way it is a sequence-modelling problem, which is exactly what self-attention is good at — hence the entire SASRec / BERT4Rec / Transformer lineage. Attention lets item it look back across the whole history and decide for itself which earlier items matter, instead of being forced through a recurrent bottleneck.

… in a continual learning setting

Here is where it gets hard, and where both papers live. In a lab, you have a fixed dataset: train, validate, test, publish. In production the data is a stream. New users sign up every second. New items are added daily. Interests drift — the user who spent March on true crime spends April on cooking shows.

You have two bad options. Retrain from scratch on the full history every time, which is correct but ruinously expensive and always stale by the time it finishes. Or fine-tune on only the new data, which is cheap but causes catastrophic forgetting: the model chases this week's behaviour and quietly discards the long-term preferences that made it good. Continual learning is the study of getting the cheap option to behave like the expensive one.

The Two Halves of the System

These two papers are worth reading together because they attack the same problem — recommendation over a data stream that never stops moving — from opposite directions. They are not two halves of one pipeline. Each is a complete, standalone method. What makes the pairing instructive is that the signal each one captures is precisely the signal the other cannot see.

CSTRec is the sequential half — a Transformer that models your trajectory through time. Its core contribution is Continual Sequential Attention (CSA), a linear attention designed for streaming data, with two components: Cauchy–Schwarz Normalisation, which stabilises training when users interact at wildly different frequencies (some watch nightly, some monthly), and Collaborative Interest Enrichment, which fights forgetting through shared learnable interest pools rather than replaying old data. That last point matters practically: it retains historical knowledge without direct access to prior data, which is a very different privacy and storage story than a replay buffer. Note what is absent: CSTRec involves no teacher, no student, and no distillation of any kind. It is one Transformer, trained continually, that emits a ranking.

CCD is the collaborative method, and this is where distillation actually lives. It is a teacher–student system: a massive teacher ensemble captures the global structure of the user–item graph, and that structure is distilled into a compact student small enough to actually serve. The distillation is entirely internal to CCD — teacher to student, both inside the same method. Both models then evolve along the stream together, with the student's confident predictions transferred back to the teacher. The collaborative backbone is the usual graph-or-autoencoder family: LightGCN-style neighbourhood propagation over the bipartite interaction graph, or a VAE-style encoder–decoder over the interaction vector.

A note on precision: the CCD paper's own experiments use MF- and GNN-based students with a large ensemble teacher. LightGCN and VAE are the natural drop-ins for that collaborative backbone slot, and that is how I am drawing it below.

Two independent pipelines over one stream. CSTRec (left) knows your order and ends at a ranking — there is no distillation in that column at all. CCD (right) knows everyone else's co-occurrence structure, and its distillation step is internal: teacher into student, so that what the ensemble learned can be served at deployable size. The dashed arrow is CCD's twist — knowledge flows back up from student to teacher too.

Why KL Divergence Is the Right Loss for Distillation

Everything in this section describes what happens inside CCD, between its teacher and its student. CSTRec plays no part here.

The obvious way to train a student is on the ground-truth labels: the user watched item 62, so push up item 62 and push down everything else. This is a one-hot target, and it throws away almost everything the teacher knows.

Because look at what the teacher's output actually contains. Over four candidates it might say Shutter Island 50%, Tenet 30%, Dunkirk 15%, Barbie 5%. The hard label only tells you the argmax. The full distribution tells you that Tenet was a close second, that Dunkirk was plausible-but-weaker, and that Barbie was never in the running. Hinton called these the dark knowledge — the relative sizes of the wrong answers encode the teacher's entire learned similarity structure over the catalogue. A one-hot label has zero bits of that. The teacher's distribution has all of it.

So you want a loss that says make the student's whole distribution look like the teacher's whole distribution. That is exactly what KL divergence measures:

DKL( q ‖ p )  =  ∑i   qi   log ( qi / pi ) q = teacher distribution (the target), p = student distribution. Always ≥ 0, and = 0 exactly when p = q.

Three properties make this the natural choice. First, it is zero if and only if the distributions match, so minimising it is literally "become the teacher." Second, it is asymmetric in a useful direction: the qi out front weights each term by how much the teacher cares. Items the teacher assigns near-zero probability contribute near-nothing, so the student is not forced to waste capacity matching the teacher's opinion about the 400,000 items that are all equally irrelevant. It concentrates the student's effort where the teacher is confident.

Third, and this is the elegant part — the gradient. If you differentiate the KL through a softmax with respect to the student's logits, essentially everything cancels and you are left with:

∂ ℒKD / ∂ zSi  =  pi − qi student probability minus teacher probability. That is the entire gradient signal.

Which reads exactly like what you want it to read: wherever the student is over-confident relative to the teacher, push down by the gap; wherever it is under-confident, push up by the gap. No special cases, no clipping, no hand-tuned per-item weighting. The size of the disagreement is the size of the correction.

The temperature knob

One practical wrinkle. A well-trained teacher is often too confident — it puts 0.98 on one item and spreads 0.02 across everything else, which is nearly one-hot again and leaks almost no dark knowledge. So you divide the logits by a temperature τ > 1 before the softmax, which flattens the distribution and makes the relative ordering of the tail visible. Since this shrinks gradients by roughly 1/τ², you multiply the loss back by τ² to keep the scale sane. That is where the τ² in the diagram comes from.

Worth knowing: CCD itself does not use the vanilla pointwise KL — it uses a list-wise distillation loss, the negative log-likelihood of the teacher's permutation probability over ranked items. The intuition is identical (match the teacher's full opinion, not just its argmax), but the object being matched is a distribution over orderings rather than over items. For recommendation that is arguably the more honest target, because ranking is what you actually ship.

Step 4 — The collaborative model (CCD)

Now hand the same user to CCD, which knows nothing about order. Its teacher runs LightGCN propagation over the interaction graph and sees something the sequential model structurally cannot: the users who watched this exact cluster overwhelmingly went on to Shutter Island — same unreliable-narrator machinery, same DiCaprio, and not a Nolan film at all. Style-matching would never surface it. Co-occurrence surfaces it immediately.

The distillation about to happen is internal to CCD: its teacher teaching its student. CSTRec is a separate model that produced a separate answer, and nothing is being distilled from it. The two distributions happen to be close, which is convenient for the illustration but is not a claim that they are the same object.

Step 5 — Distillation, inside CCD

Compute the divergence between CCD's teacher and CCD's student, term by term:

DKL(q ‖ p) = 0.304 ln(0.304/0.306) + 0.151 ln(0.151/0.231) + 0.500 ln(0.500/0.287) + 0.045 ln(0.045/0.176)
= −0.002  −  0.064  +  0.278  −  0.062  =  0.150 nats Individual terms can be negative; the total is guaranteed non-negative.

Look at where the mass of that loss sits. The Tenet term is −0.002 — teacher and student already agree, so no gradient. The Shutter Island term is +0.278, carrying essentially the entire loss, because that is the one place the student is badly wrong in a direction the teacher is confident about. Applying the gradient p − q pushes Shutter Island up hard, Barbie and Dunkirk down, and leaves Tenet alone.

And they disagree — which is the entire point. CSTRec says Tenet because Tenet is more Nolan, and this user's trajectory is Nolan-shaped. CCD says Shutter Island because people who watched precisely these four films went there next, a fact no amount of style-matching will recover. Neither is wrong. They are answering from different evidence, and a production system wants both signals available rather than one of them thrown away.

Continual Learning: What Changes When Data Is a Stream

Everything above was a single forward pass at a single moment. Continual learning is about what happens to that model over the following six months.

The standard experimental protocol — used by both papers, on Gowalla (location check-ins), ML-1M (MovieLens ratings), and Yelp (business reviews) — is to sort every interaction by timestamp and cut it into blocks. The first 50% becomes the base block, used for a full pretrain. The remaining 50% is split into five incremental blocks of 10% each, fed to the model one at a time, as if they were arriving live.

The strict temporal ordering is the point. You never let the model see the future, which sounds obvious but is violated constantly by random train/test splits in recommender papers. And after each block you evaluate on both axes:

  • Knowledge acquisition — how well does the model predict interactions in the block it just learned? Did it actually absorb the new data?
  • Knowledge retention — how well does it still predict earlier blocks? Or did learning April cost it everything it knew about March?

Naive fine-tuning wins the first and loses the second, badly. Full retraining wins both and costs a fortune. Every technique in both papers — CSA's shared interest pools, CCD's proxy-guided replay — exists to buy retention without buying a full retrain.

Why production runs on fine-tuning, not retraining

In a paper, "retrain from scratch" is a baseline row in a table. In production it is a non-starter, for reasons that compound:

  • Cost scales with total history, not new data. Each retrain re-processes years of interactions to absorb one day of new ones. Fine-tuning scales with what actually arrived. On a catalogue with hundreds of millions of interactions that is not a 2× difference, it is orders of magnitude.
  • Staleness is measured in hours, and hours matter. Take Prime Video. Someone starts a series tonight; the recommendation that matters is the one on tomorrow morning's home row. A weekly retrain cadence means the system is reasoning about a version of you that is up to seven days out of date — which, for a user mid-binge, is the difference between the obviously-right recommendation and a baffling one.
  • Cold-start items have no history to retrain on. A show that premiered yesterday has almost no interaction data. Waiting for the next full retrain means it sits invisible during precisely the window when promotion matters most. This is why CCD's entity embedding initialisation — seeding new items from their 1-hop and 2-hop graph neighbours — is doing real work rather than being a footnote.
  • Interest capture is the actual product. Streaming platforms live and die on how fast they detect a shift in taste. A household that just had a kid, a user who moved from comedies to documentaries — the value is in noticing within days, not quarters.

So the real question was never "fine-tune or retrain." It is: given that you are going to fine-tune, how do you stop the model forgetting? That is the question both papers answer, and it is the question I spent my internship inside.

An Aside on KANs

Kolmogorov–Arnold Networks are a rethinking of where the learnable parts of a network should live. A standard MLP puts fixed activation functions on the nodes (ReLU, GELU — you pick one and it never changes) and learnable scalar weights on the edges. KANs inherit their structure from the Kolmogorov–Arnold representation theorem, which says any multivariate continuous function can be written as a finite composition of continuous univariate functions and addition — and they invert the MLP arrangement accordingly: the edges carry learnable univariate functions, typically parameterised as B-splines, and the nodes do nothing but sum. So instead of learning "how much of this input to pass through a fixed curve," the network learns the curve itself, per connection. The practical claims are better parameter efficiency on smooth low-dimensional functions and much better interpretability — you can literally plot the function on any edge and read what the network learned, which for scientific problems sometimes recovers the underlying symbolic formula. The costs are real too: training is slower, the spline machinery does not exploit modern GPU kernels the way dense matmuls do, and the advantage over a well-tuned MLP narrows considerably as dimensionality grows. Interesting rather than settled — which is exactly why it is worth having in the toolkit.
